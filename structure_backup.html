How does it happen ?

Follow the mosquito to know

Mosquitoes can transmit illnesses to humans and other animals, known as vector-borne diseases [1]. They are part of a group of vectors, including ticks and fleas, which carry pathogens like viruses, bacteria, and parasites.

Among 3,578 mosquito species, 331 species are identified as implicated in human transmission [2]

Half of the world's population lives in a region at risk of a disease transmited by mosquitoes [1]

Among them, mosquitoes can transmit several diseases. The main one are malaria, dengue, Zika virus, chikungunya, West Nile virus, yellow fever, and Japanese encephalitis.

1. A healthy human gets bitten by a mosquito carrying a disease.

2. The pathogens reproduce and multiply inside the human, and symptoms appear.

3. A healthy mosquito bites the sick human and carries the disease.

4. The pathogens reproduce inside the mosquito.

5. The sick mosquito bites another human and continues the transmission to multiple humans.

What if a sick human go in a zone with different species of mosquitoes ?

6. The species of mosquitoes in this new zone is different. The mosquito reproduces the pathogen but cannot spread it.

How does it spread?

A disease can spread from one person to another through a mosquito bite, as the mosquito transfers pathogens into the person's blood. These pathogens multiply in both the human and the mosquito, but not directly between humans.

How was it in the past?

Malaria and dengue are among the most significant vector-borne diseases, transmitted by Anopheles and Aedes mosquitoes, respectively. Over time, these diseases have spread across various countries while also disappearing from certain regions.

2000 2021

What influences the spread?

Numerous studies (2), motivated by public health concerns, have examined potential scenarios for the future spread of diseases. These studies have focused on the environmental conditions favorable to the reproduction of the mosquito species Aedes Albopictus, which was introduced to Europe in the 1970s and is capable of transmitting diseases such as dengue.

Climate change

As global temperatures rise, previously too cold regions may become suitable for mosquito habitation. Additionally, increased rainfall can create more stagnant water pools for mosquito breeding.

Globalization

Global travel and trade make it easier for mosquitoes to spread. They can travel in cargo, vehicles, and aircraft, reaching new places. Increased trade also means mosquito eggs or larvae can be transported in goods, helping them colonize new areas.

Urban area

Urban areas are ideal for mosquitoes due to higher temperatures, abundant water containers and irrigation systems, and a high density of human hosts.

How to protect ourselves?

Protecting ourselves from mosquitoes will become more and more important. Adopting new behaviors will help to reduce the spread of the disease by diminishing the population of mosquitoes. This will also have an impact on our community and will be the responsibility of everyone.

Use repellents

Apply insect repellent containing DEET, picaridin to exposed skin and clothing.

Wear protective clothing

Wear long sleeves, long pants, and socks to protect your skin.

Use mosquito nets

Sleep under mosquito and install panels on your windows and doors

Eliminate standing water

Regularly empty or cover containers such as buckets, plant pots, and old tires.

Treat water source

Use larvicides in standing water that can't be eliminated, like ponds and rain barrels.

Support control programs

Participate in local mosquito control programs and spread awareness about prevention measures.
